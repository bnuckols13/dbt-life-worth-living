Existential Group Curriculum

Life Worth Living

Six sessions on freedom, finitude, isolation, meaning, authenticity, and suffering

About this curriculum

This curriculum does not offer answers. It offers better questions, and the courage to sit with them honestly. Each session explores one of the core existential concerns identified by Irvin Yalom, Viktor Frankl, and Jean-Paul Sartre, applied directly to the experience of living with and recovering from an eating disorder.

These sessions can be run in order or as standalone groups. Each follows the same structure: opening, learning, practice, and integration.

Confidentiality

What's shared here stays here. Names, stories, details. All protected. The one exception is safety: if there's risk of harm, that changes things. Outside of that, this space is yours.

Session I

Freedom

The terror and gift of choice

Opening

8 to 10 minWelcome

Today we're talking about a concept that sounds simple and turns out to be terrifying: you are free. You have always been free. Even now.

Passes are always okay, on any question, at any point. Listening counts as full participation.

1. Bridge question

Is there a part of your recovery, or your life, where you've been treating a choice as if it's not really yours to make?

Quick round. One sentence each, or just a nod. The goal is to land the theme before the learning section does the heavier lifting.

2. Experiential exercise

Close your eyes or soften your gaze. Think of one decision you've been avoiding. Not a big one. Something small that you've been treating as already decided.

1
Notice what happens in your body when you acknowledge: this is actually a choice.
Tension, relief, resistance, fear. Just notice.
2
Notice what you've been telling yourself about why it isn't a choice.
"I can't," "I have to," "there's no other option."
3
Open your eyes. That tension between freedom and avoidance is exactly what we're looking at today.
Learning

15 to 20 minFreedom and responsibility

"We are condemned to be free. Existence precedes essence." Jean-Paul Sartre

Sartre's core claim is radical: there is no predetermined nature, no cosmic script, no authority telling you who to be. You exist first. Then you create yourself through choices. All of them.

Bad faith
+

Sartre's term for when we pretend we have no choice. Bad faith is not lying to others. It's lying to yourself about your own freedom.

  • "I can't help it, that's just who I am"
  • "I don't have a choice in this"
  • "The disorder makes me do it"
  • "I'd change, but I can't"
Bad faith protects against the terror of being fully responsible for your own life, the grief of past choices, and the exposure of admitting you want something different.
Response-ability (Frankl)
+

Viktor Frankl, writing from inside a concentration camp, introduced the concept of response-ability: not the absence of difficult circumstances, but the ability to choose your response to them.

  • You did not choose your trauma, your neurology, your family, your disorder's origins
  • You did not choose to suffer
  • You do choose, every day, what you do next
  • Both are true. Neither cancels the other
The responsibility piece can land as blame if not handled carefully. Hold both sides: the circumstances were real, the pain is real, AND the agency is real.
The disorder as escape from freedom
+

There is no escape from freedom, only the illusion of escape. The eating disorder is one of those illusions. It offers the feeling of having no choice, which is temporarily less terrifying than having all the choices.

The goal is not to shame anyone for past choices. It's to open the door to future ones.
Practice

15 minThe choice audit

Six domains of life. Each one holds a common "bad faith" pattern. Click to reveal the pattern and the question underneath.

Recovery
+

The pattern: "Recovery has to happen on my terms or not at all." Using conditions to make the choice disappear.

Where are you adding conditions that make the choice disappear?
Relationships
+

The pattern: "I can't reach out, they'll think less of me." Fear dressed up as inevitability.

What would you risk if you let yourself choose connection?
Identity
+

The pattern: "I don't know who I am without this." Making the disorder permanent by treating it as essential.

What identity are you avoiding by keeping this one?
Body
+

The pattern: "I can't trust my body, it always lies." Outsourcing bodily authority to rules.

What would it mean to take responsibility for one bodily choice today?
Future
+

The pattern: "I'll deal with that when I'm better." Using a future threshold to avoid present choosing.

What are you waiting for permission to want?
Daily life
+

The pattern: "I don't have time / energy / support to do it differently." Circumstances as excuse rather than context.

In this specific moment, what choice is actually available to you?
Discussion

Draw a prompt

Tap a card to flip it. Use whatever comes up, or draw another.

◆Draw
◆Draw
◆Draw
◆Draw
Integration

5 to 7 minThings worth carrying out

◆You are not your circumstances. You are what you do with them.
◆Bad faith is not a character flaw. It's a coping strategy. Naming it is the first move toward something different.
◆Freedom is not the same as ease. Hard choices are still choices.
◆The disorder has been making some of your choices. You can take them back. Not all at once, but one at a time.
Facilitator closing

We've been sitting with a hard idea today: that freedom is real, and that it belongs to you even when it doesn't feel like it does. I don't want to pretend that's comfortable. It isn't. But it's also the only thing that makes recovery possible. Take a moment before you leave. Notice one choice that's yours this week. Something small. You don't have to make it differently yet. Just notice it's there.

Weekly practice

Once this week, catch yourself saying "I have no choice about this." Pause and ask: is that actually true? What am I protecting myself from by believing it? This doesn't require action. Just noticing.

Session II

Death

Your finite, precious life

Opening

8 to 10 minWelcome

Today's session is about death. Not as a morbid topic, but as a fact that changes everything once you look at it honestly. Most of us work very hard not to look.

This topic can bring up unexpected emotion. Passes are always available. There's no requirement to go anywhere you're not ready to go.

1. Bridge question

When you imagine the end of your life, not right now but eventually, what do you most hope to have done, or been, or felt?

2. Experiential exercise

Before we begin. Take a breath. Notice that you're here, alive, in this room, right now.

1
Feel the weight of your body in the chair. The temperature of the air.
Simple facts of being alive, right now.
2
Notice one thing you can see, one thing you can hear, one thing you can feel.
Presence. Not performance.
3
Hold this for a moment: you are here. That is not nothing.
Learning

15 to 20 minDeath and finitude

"Death is the condition that makes it possible to live authentically." Irvin Yalom

The existentialists were not morbid for thinking about death constantly. They thought about it because it's the one fact that makes everything else matter. Your time is finite. That means it has weight.

Being-toward-death (Heidegger)
+

Most of us live in a state of "falling," absorbed in the everyday, distracted from the fact that we are going to die. This absorption is comfortable, but it costs us our authenticity.

When death becomes real to us, not abstract but personally real, it can function as an awakening experience. Suddenly, we see what we've been wasting time on.

  • Which relationships are worth fighting for
  • Which fears are worth walking through
  • What you've been waiting for permission to want
  • How much of your life the disorder has already borrowed
Immortality projects (Becker)
+

Ernest Becker's insight: most human striving is an unconscious attempt to transcend mortality. We build "immortality projects" that make us feel permanent, significant, invincible.

  • Control over the body as control over vulnerability itself
  • Perfection as a defense against the randomness of being mortal
  • Shrinking as a way to feel somehow less subject to death
  • Rules as a substitute for the security that impermanence denies
The "immortality project" frame is often very resonant. Give space for recognition. Some may push back. That's also worth exploring.
Practice

15 minThe finitude exercise

Three reflection prompts. Allow silence after each. These are invitations, not questions to be answered quickly.

Prompt I
+

If you had one year to live, with no physical limitations, no financial constraints, just your life as it is plus one year: what would you do with it? What would you stop doing?

Prompt II
+

At the end of your life, what do you think you would regret most? Not what you should regret. What do you actually think you would?

Prompt III
+

How much of your finite time has the disorder already taken? Not to create guilt. Just to let that be real for a moment.

Discussion
Opening
What was it like to sit with these prompts? What came up?
Deeper
Is there something you've been putting off that actually matters to you? What's keeping it in the future?
Reframe
What would change in how you approach today, just today, if you held clearly that your time is limited?
Integration

5 to 7 minThings worth carrying out

◆Your life is finite. That's not a tragedy. It's what makes it matter.
◆Control over the body is not the same as control over mortality. The illusion costs more than it's worth.
◆What you regret not doing is usually not less scary than what you did. It's just a different kind of grief.
◆Wasting time is something you actually have the power to stop doing. Not all of it. Some of it.
Facilitator closing

We went somewhere today that most people avoid. I want to acknowledge that. You thought about your own death, or at least the edge of it. That takes a particular kind of courage. Practice this week: let yourself notice one moment, just one, where you feel the preciousness of being alive. It doesn't have to be a big moment. It just has to be real.

Session III

Isolation

Alone together

Opening

8 to 10 minWelcome

Today we're exploring something almost everyone in this room knows well: the particular aloneness that lives inside an eating disorder, and what it has to do with a bigger fact about being human.

This topic can touch loneliness that runs deep. You don't owe anyone access to that today.

1. Bridge question

Name one person, past or present, with whom you have felt genuinely less alone. Not perfectly known. Just less alone. What made that possible?

2. Experiential exercise

Look around the room for a moment. Make brief eye contact with one person. Just a second.

1
Notice what happened in your body when you made eye contact.
Warmth, discomfort, avoidance, something else.
2
Notice any urge to look away, and what that urge is protecting.
3
That tension between wanting to be seen and wanting to hide is the center of today's session.
Learning

15 to 20 minIsolation and connection

"The price we pay for consciousness is that we are forever alone." Irvin Yalom

Yalom identified three distinct types of isolation, each one real and each one calling for a different response.

Interpersonal isolation
+

Loneliness in the ordinary sense. Lack of connection with others.

  • The disorder creates and deepens this: secrecy, shame, rituals that isolate
  • Often the most visible type, the one people name when they say they're lonely
Intrapersonal isolation
+

Disconnection from parts of yourself: emotions, desires, memories, body signals.

  • The disorder accelerates this: numbness, dissociation, rules that override inner experience
  • Often invisible from the outside, but felt as an inner blankness or strangeness
Existential isolation
+

The unbridgeable gap: no one can fully occupy your consciousness, or you theirs. This is not pathology. It's the human condition.

  • The paradox: accepting existential isolation is the precondition for genuine connection
  • When you stop demanding to be fully known, you can actually be met
The deep aloneness of the eating disorder is not just a symptom. It's an existential problem wearing a clinical costume.
Practice

15 minThe three rooms

Three types of isolation, three reflection spaces. Open each one.

Room I: Interpersonal
+

The loneliness you can see from the outside.

Who are you keeping at a distance right now, and why? Not who you should be close to. Who you actually are pushing away, and what the disorder has to do with that.
Room II: Intrapersonal
+

The parts of yourself you've lost access to.

Which parts of you have gone quiet: feelings, wants, physical sensations, memories? Is there a part of your inner life the disorder has taken the most from?
Room III: Existential
+

The aloneness that can't be fixed, only accepted.

Is there a way you've been demanding to be fully known, by a partner, a parent, a therapist, and experiencing the inevitable gap as rejection? What might it look like to let the gap be real, and still connect across it?
Discussion
Opening
Which room are you most familiar with? Which one surprised you?
Deeper
What does the disorder give you in exchange for the isolation it creates? What is it protecting you from?
Toward connection
What would a small move toward less isolation look like for you? Not perfectly connected. Just less alone.
Integration

5 to 7 minThings worth carrying out

◆The disorder's isolation is not the same as the existential aloneness. One can be addressed. One has to be accepted.
◆You cannot be fully known. That's not a failure of whoever is trying. It's just true.
◆Being partially known, genuinely, imperfectly known, is not a consolation prize. It's what's actually available.
◆The parts of yourself you've been keeping in the dark still exist. They're waiting, not gone.
Facilitator closing

Isolation is one of the things the disorder does best. It makes a world of one, where the only relationship that matters is the one between you and the rules. Practice this week: notice one moment when you're in the presence of another person and feel less alone, even briefly. Just notice it.

Session IV

Meaning

Where meaning lives

Opening

8 to 10 minWelcome

Viktor Frankl survived Auschwitz. What he found there, and what he built from it, is the foundation of today's session. Meaning is not given. It is found, and it is made.

Passes are always available. This topic can touch places of real despair and real aliveness. Both are welcome here.

1. Bridge question

What's something you've done, experienced, or survived that felt genuinely meaningful, even if it was painful? What made it feel that way?

2. Experiential exercise

Close your eyes. Think of a moment in your life, any moment, where you felt like things mattered. It doesn't have to be grand.

1
Where were you? What were you doing?
Let the memory be specific. A place, a person, a sensation.
2
What made that moment feel meaningful?
Connection? Creation? Endurance? Something else entirely?
3
Open your eyes. That source of meaning is still available to you. We're going to talk about why.
Learning

15 to 20 minMeaning and purpose

"He who has a why to live can bear almost any how." Nietzsche, cited by Frankl

Frankl developed logotherapy from his observation in the camps: those with a reason to survive were more likely to survive. The eating disorder is a meaning system. It provides structure, purpose, identity, and direction. Recovery is not just removing that system. It's building a better one.

Creative values: what you give
+

Meaning found in creating, contributing, building something.

  • Work, art, caregiving, problem-solving. Anything you make or offer
  • The disorder often attacks this: "nothing I make is good enough"
Experiential values: what you receive
+

Meaning found in beauty, love, truth, connection. What you encounter.

  • A piece of music, a person, a landscape, a moment of understanding
  • The disorder often blocks this: rules and numbness close off receptivity
Attitudinal values: how you face what can't be changed
+

Meaning found in the stance you take toward unavoidable suffering. The "last human freedom": no one can take your attitude from you.

  • This is the source available even when the others aren't
  • Frankl never romanticized suffering. Attitudinal meaning is not "everything happens for a reason." It's more honest than that
Practice

15 minThe meaning compass

What you give
+

What do you make, create, offer, or contribute that feels real to you, even in a small way?

Examples: care for others, a skill, a thing made, a problem solved, a moment you showed up.
What you receive
+

What have you loved, encountered, or been moved by? What has made you feel the world is worth being in?

Examples: music that hit you at the right moment, someone who truly saw you, a landscape, a text that changed something.
How you face it
+

Is there something you've faced, that you didn't choose and couldn't change, where you found a way to hold yourself with dignity?

Examples: continuing treatment when it's hard, being honest when it cost you something, staying present when everything in you wanted to leave.
Discussion
Opening
Which source felt most alive for you? Which felt most blocked right now?
Deeper
What does the disorder give you as a meaning substitute, and what does it cost you to keep taking that deal?
Toward meaning
If you were living more fully from one of these three sources, which one, and what would that look like in practice?
Integration

5 to 7 minThings worth carrying out

◆Meaning is not found by looking for it directly. It shows up as a byproduct of engaging with something beyond yourself.
◆The disorder offers a meaning structure. Recovery requires building a better one, not just dismantling the old one.
◆Even when creative and experiential meaning feel out of reach, the attitudinal source is always available.
◆Frankl did not romanticize suffering. Neither should we. But dignity in difficulty is real, and it counts.
Facilitator closing

I'm asking you to look at what's meaningful to you. Not what should be meaningful, but what actually is. That's hard to see when the disorder has been filling that space. But it's there.

Session V

Authenticity

The gap within

Opening

8 to 10 minWelcome

There is a version of you that exists inside, and a version that gets shown to the world. Today we look at the gap between them. Not to close it all at once, but to see it clearly.

This can bring up vulnerability and shame. Passes are available. You don't have to expose anything you're not ready to.

1. Bridge question

Is there a part of yourself that almost no one gets to see? Not a shameful secret. Just a real part of you that lives mostly in private. What would it mean for that part to exist more openly?

2. Experiential exercise

Think of the last social interaction you had before coming here today.

1
How much of what you were actually feeling did you let show?
A percentage. No judgment. Just an honest estimate.
2
What did you edit out? What stayed hidden?
3
Notice: the gap between inside and outside is today's subject. You're already living in it.
Learning

15 to 20 minAuthenticity and self-deception

"Authenticity is a relational good. It requires both the courage to be yourself and the courage to let others see you." adapted from Charles Taylor

The eating disorder creates and maintains the gap. It offers a performed identity that feels safer than the real one. Recovery is, in part, the willingness to let the real one be seen, even imperfectly.

What the false self protects against
+
  • The terror of being known and still rejected
  • The grief of having hidden for so long
  • The vulnerability of wanting things openly
  • The responsibility that comes with being fully present
What authentic living asks for
+
  • Letting your actual emotional state be visible, not performing calm or OK
  • Saying what you actually want, not what you think others want to hear
  • Allowing preferences, opinions, and desires to exist without apologizing for them
  • Tolerating the discomfort of being seen while still imperfect
Authenticity is not about finding some perfect inner self. It's about narrowing the distance between what you experience and what you allow to show.
Practice

15 minThe gap map

For each domain, notice the distance between what you feel inside and what you show. This is not about judgment. It's about seeing clearly.

1
Emotions
Inside: what you actually feel. Outside: what you let people see.
2
Desires
Inside: what you actually want. Outside: what you say you want.
3
Recovery
Inside: where you actually are. Outside: what you report.
4
Relationships
Inside: what you need. Outside: what you ask for.
5
Identity
Inside: who you feel like. Outside: who you perform as.
Discussion
Opening
Where is the gap widest for you right now? What makes it hard to close?
Deeper
What would you lose if you let people see the real version? What might you gain?
One small move
What is one place this week where you could narrow the gap, even slightly?
Integration

5 to 7 minThings worth carrying out

◆The gap is not a moral failing. It's a survival strategy that may have outlived its usefulness.
◆You do not have to close the gap all at once. One inch is enough for today.
◆Being seen imperfectly is not the same as being seen and rejected. Most people can hold more of you than you think.
◆The disorder thrives in the gap. It needs the false self to stay in charge. Authenticity is quietly subversive.
Facilitator closing

We've been looking at the space between inside and outside today. That space is not empty. It's full of fear and longing and protection and grief. Closing it is not about being brave once. It's about small, repeated acts of letting yourself be seen. Practice: one moment this week where you let the inside show a little more than usual. That's it.

Session VI

Suffering

The phoenix story

Opening

8 to 10 minWelcome

This is the final session. Everything we've explored, freedom, finitude, isolation, meaning, authenticity, converges here. Suffering is the common thread. The question is what you do with it.

This session can bring up real pain. Passes are always available. You have gotten this far. That is not nothing.

1. Bridge question

What is the hardest thing you have survived? Not necessarily the disorder. Anything. And what, if anything, did surviving it teach you about yourself?

2. Experiential exercise

Place both feet on the floor. Feel the ground.

1
Think of one thing you have endured that you didn't choose. Let it be real.
2
Now notice: you are here. You came through it. That's a fact about you.
3
Hold both at the same time: the weight of what happened, and the fact that you're still here. Today's session lives in that space.
Learning

15 to 20 minSuffering and transformation

"When we are no longer able to change a situation, we are challenged to change ourselves." Viktor Frankl

Frankl's attitudinal values come full circle here: the last human freedom is your stance toward what cannot be changed. Suffering is not redeemed by a lesson. It is endured with whatever dignity you can find. That dignity is real, and it belongs to you.

What suffering is not
+
  • Not a test you pass or fail
  • Not something that "happens for a reason"
  • Not proof that you're broken or undeserving
  • Not something you should be "over" by now
What suffering can be
+
  • A place where you discover your own resilience
  • A context that clarifies what actually matters
  • A source of depth, compassion, and hard-won wisdom
  • Something that does not have to define you, even as it marks you
The phoenix is not the fire. It's what comes through the fire. You are not your suffering. You are what you do with it.
Integrating all six sessions
+
  • Freedom: you have choices, even in suffering
  • Death: your time is limited, which gives suffering urgency and weight
  • Isolation: suffering is lonely, but you are not the only one
  • Meaning: suffering can be endured if it serves something
  • Authenticity: suffering asks you to be honest about what's real
Practice

15 minThe phoenix letter

Write a short letter. You can write it in your head, out loud, or on paper. The letter is to yourself, from the version of you that has come through this. Not the version that's "fixed." The version that has endured.

1
What do you want to say to yourself about what you've survived?
2
What do you know now that you didn't know before?
3
What do you want to carry forward? What do you want to leave behind?

You don't have to share any of this. If you want to read a sentence, a phrase, or a word to the group, you can. Pass is always here.

Discussion
Opening
What was it like to write to yourself from that place?
Across all six sessions
Which session stays with you the most? Freedom, death, isolation, meaning, authenticity, suffering. Which one changed something?
Forward
What are you taking with you from this curriculum? Not what you should take. What actually landed.
Integration

5 to 7 minThings worth carrying out

◆Suffering is not meaningful because it hurts. It can become meaningful because of what you do with it.
◆You are not the fire. You are what comes through it.
◆The life worth living is not the one without suffering. It's the one where suffering is met with honesty, choice, connection, meaning, and the willingness to keep being real.
◆You have gotten this far. That is not nothing.
Facilitator closing

This has been a six-session conversation about the hardest things in human life. Freedom, death, isolation, meaning, authenticity, suffering. You sat with all of them. You didn't have to do that, and you did. I want you to know: the willingness to look at these things honestly is itself an act of courage. It is itself a life worth living. Thank you for being here. All six times, or however many times you showed up. It counted. Every time.
